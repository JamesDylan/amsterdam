01 · Strategy

Growth Loops Elevator Pitch

Adding a second user to a company drops churn from ~60% to ~40%. A company with 5+ more bookings churns at ~25%, against 70% for a single booking. Today we pull these levers one at a time. Growth Loops wires them together. A user recruits the arranger. The arranger grows the team. The team books across more content types. More bookings lower churn and feed the incentive that recruits the next user. The number that tracks this is K-factor, the number of new users you get for each user acquired.

Evidence from DSA company analysis 2025 and B4B churn cohorts
Working definition · Growth loop
"The test for a loop is simple. A funnel ends when the user reaches the bottom. A loop returns the user to the top as a recruiter, a more frequent booker, or a wider-basket booker, so the next turn starts from a larger base."
02 · The structure

Two layers: People loops and Value loops

The four loops do two different jobs. Two add more people to a company, because more users lower churn and raise booking volume. Two add more value per user, because more bookings and more content types make the company stickier. The two layers feed each other.

People loops More users per company. Each new user becomes a way to bring in the next.
01
Recruit the arranger
A solo booker invites the person who really arranges their travel. That arranger then books for them and for others, and each of those others can recruit an arranger too.
02
Grow the team around the arranger
The arranger adds the people they book for with a name and email. Those travellers connect their own colleagues, and the company gets larger from the inside.
→ then each new user gives us more to work with →
Value loops More value per user. Each booking creates a reason to come back and book again.
03
Widen the basket
A user books a hotel, then adds the matching car, taxi, flight, rail, attraction, or meeting room. The trip now sits in one place, so the next trip starts in B4B.
04
Build the booking habit
The more a company books, the less it churns. We make the next booking easier than the last and prompt it at the right time, so B4B becomes the default.

01 People loop · More users

Recruit the arranger

A solo booker names the person who really arranges their travel and sends a soft invite. That arranger then books for them and for others. Each of those others can name their own arranger, so the loop keeps producing new arrangers.

Solo company churn ~60% Arrangers: 15% of users, 82% of bookings Arrangers activate at 30% vs 11% solo
Why this matters

The solo booker is our lowest-value user. They book for themselves, churn at about 60%, and rarely see the company-level value. But many of them are not the person who actually arranges the travel. Their EA, team admin, or manager's PA does. When we ask the solo booker to name that person and send a soft invite, we move the account to our highest-value user in one step.

The loop closes because the recruited arranger then adds the people they book for, and each of those travellers can name their own arranger. One low-value account becomes a source of new arrangers.

02 People loop · More users

Grow the team around the arranger

The arranger adds the people they book for with a name and email. Those travellers get booked for straight away. When they see colleagues already on B4B, they connect, and the company gets larger.

1 to 2 users: churn 60% to 40% Each profile = ~4 to 8 bookings 83% of companies have guest travellers
Why this matters

The arranger is the engine for team growth. They add travellers with a name and an email, and we send a soft invite so booking can start before the traveller accepts. Each added traveller gives the arranger another reason to book on B4B. Some of those travellers already have colleagues sitting on separate B4B accounts. A prompt to connect brings those accounts into one company.

The loop closes because every member added is another node that can pull in the next colleague. The company keeps growing from inside, without us paying to acquire each new person.

03 Value loop · More per user

Widen the basket

A user books a hotel. We offer the matching car, taxi, flight, rail, attraction, or meeting room for the same trip. Once a trip holds more than one content type, B4B becomes the place the user starts the next trip.

Hotels today, six content types soon Punch-outs: taxi, car, attractions, meeting rooms Flights and rail in build
Why this matters

Most companies book only hotels on B4B. Each extra content type is a new reason to come back. When a booking user, a self-booker or an arranger, adds a car or a flight to a hotel trip, the whole trip sits in one place in B4B. A consolidated itinerary then becomes the obvious place to start the next trip, because the last one is already there.

The loop closes because each content type added raises the cost of booking the trip anywhere else. The next trip is more likely to start in B4B, and it picks up more content types again.

04 Value loop · More per user

Build the booking habit

The more a company books, the less it churns. We make the next booking easier than the last and prompt it at the right time. Booking on B4B becomes the default, and frequency feeds the other three loops.

1 booking company: ~70% churn 5+ booking company: ~25% churn Frequency fuels the other loops
Why this matters

Churn falls fast as booking count rises. A one-booking company churns at about 70%. A company with five or more bookings churns at about 25%. When we learn a user's travel pattern, we can prompt the next trip before they go elsewhere. Repeat and draft bookings cut the effort of booking again.

The loop closes because each booking lowers the effort and raises the reward of the next, since frequency moves the company up the Genius tiers. Frequency is also the fuel for the other loops. A more frequent arranger adds more travellers and books more content types.

05 · Incentives

Incentives trigger the loops. They are not a loop.

Genius tiers and team rewards are not part of Growth Loops. But they give a user a concrete reason to take the loop action now. The dual-path Genius card sets two ways to reach Level 3, one through team size and one through booking frequency. A prompt that colleagues are already on B4B turns connecting them into a way to climb a tier. This panel shows where each incentive plugs in.

07 · Why this approach

Compared to the other options we considered

A direct look at what each option does well, what it does not, and why the recommendation wins.

Option
What it does well
Why it does not work for Bcom
One Growth theme
all loops in one box
Simplest to present. One headline, one story.
It hides the two engines that actually drive growth. More users and more value per user are different jobs with different metrics. A single box reads as a grab-bag of nudges, and there is no way to show that the parts feed each other.
Organise by actor
self-booker / arranger / company
Maps cleanly to the personas we already talk about.
It overlaps the Activate and Company Management sections, which are already cut by persona and company stage. It mixes who takes the action with what the action does, and it gives no single number to track the whole story.
Funnel stages
acquire / activate / retain / refer
A familiar shape that most stakeholders recognise.
Acquire and activate are already owned by the Activate section, so this repeats it. A stage model shows a one-way path. It does not show that a retained, high-frequency user becomes a recruiter again, which is the whole point of a loop.
Recommendation
2 People + 2 ValueSHIP
The two-layer structure matches the two ways a company compounds. Why these four: two loops add users, where the churn drop is largest, and two loops add value per user, which lowers churn again and funds the next turn. K-factor ties all four together.
Watch out: most punch-out features score zero today and are not built, so position content breadth as sequenced. Be ready to name the reinvestment step for each loop, because that is what separates this from a list of nudges.
08 · Pre-meeting prep

Questions Bcom will ask. Practise the answers.

Each of these will be asked in some form. How confidently we answer them shows how strong the framing is. Do not enter the meeting without a prepared answer to all six.

How is this different from the Activate and Company sections? Are you counting the same work twice?
Those sections build the mechanisms. Activate gets a user registered and into a company. Company Management adds the second user and the team structure. Growth Loops is the system that connects them so they feed each other. The same invite feature appears here as a loop step, not as new scope. We are describing how the parts compound, not adding parts.
Prove these are loops and not funnels.
Each loop has a named reinvestment step. A recruited arranger surfaces new travellers who can recruit again. Each member added can pull in the next. Each content type makes B4B the start point for the next trip. Each booking lowers the effort of the next and moves the company up the tiers. If the step did not return the user to the top, we would call it a funnel and drop it.
What is the single metric that ties this together?
K-factor, the new active users generated per active user. Secondary metrics are content types booked per company and bookings per company per month. The churn numbers sit underneath. The loops work because more users and more bookings both lower churn, which keeps the loops running.
Widening the basket sounds like upsell. Where is the loop?
The cross-sell is the trigger. The loop is the consolidated itinerary. Once a trip holds a hotel and a car and a flight in one place, the next trip starts in B4B because the last one is there. Then the basket widens again. Upsell happens once. This repeats.
Most punch-out features are unscored or not built. Is this real for Q3?
Flights, car, and rail are the near-term basket wideners and they have real ICE scores. Taxis, attractions, and meeting rooms are at the discovery edge and score zero today. The Q3 investment is the loop mechanic, the contextual cross-sell and the consolidated itinerary. Content breadth fills in over the following quarters.
How does this connect to what Bcom invests in on their side?
Two connections. (1) Incentives: Genius tiers are the shared currency that triggers the team and frequency loops, and Bcom owns that mechanic. (2) Adjacent supply: the punch-outs ride Bcom inventory and partnerships. Both are joint roadmap items, not B4B asks.
