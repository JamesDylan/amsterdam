01 · Strategy

Arranger Elevator Pitch

Arrangers are 15% of our users. They make 82% of our bookings. They activate at 30% against 13% for self-bookers, and the top ones book 10 times as often, 29 bookings a year against 2.9. We cannot buy more of them. Arrangers do not search for themselves, so ads reach self-bookers instead. So the product has to do the work that marketing cannot. It has to find arrangers in the base we already have, turn self-bookers into arrangers, and keep the ones we have booking. This section covers the flows built for them, plus one rule. We measure and prioritise on the arranger first.

02 · The structure

Two layers: cut the effort, build the confidence

Arrangers have two problems. The work takes too long, and they are not sure they are doing it right. The part-time arranger who fell into the role feels both. Two themes cut the effort of arranging travel. Two themes give the arranger the confidence to hand work off and own the result.

01 Effort · Less work

Delegate without losing control

The arranger is buried in booking work that should be shared. Hand the booking to the traveller with a budget and guardrails set in advance. The arranger keeps control, and the company gains a second active user.

Delegation creates a 2nd active user Bookings per user rise as users rise Saves ~30 min per booking
Why this matters

Two people are stuck. The CEO or VP books only because they hold the company card, and they do not want the job. The real arranger is drowning in booking work that should be distributed. Pre-approved budgets and policy guardrails let the arranger hand a booking to the traveller without losing oversight. Profiles have to be ready first, so we populate them as the arranger books, from a spreadsheet upload, from past trips, and from contextual save prompts.

Every delegation creates a second active user. Bookings per company rise as the number of users rises, so this theme feeds growth as well as saving the arranger time.

02 Effort · Less work

Run the whole trip in one place

The arranger sets up a trip, then manages every booking inside it. Smart search gets them to the right hotel fast. Quick rebook handles the trips they run again. Travellers can book within the trip the arranger set up.

Property to booking 6.1%, target 10% Top arrangers book 29 times a year Group trips leak to email today
Why this matters

The arranger's day is one trip after another, often the same trips again. Property-to-booking conversion is 6.1% against a 10% target, so search is where the time goes. Smart filters and a clear top-three comparison get them to the right hotel faster. Quick rebook turns a repeat trip into one action. Group and event bookings, which leak to email and spreadsheets today, sit in one container with a view of who has booked.

The search work overlaps the Property Details work already in flight, so we deconflict rather than build in parallel. One place to set up, book, and manage a trip is the surface the arranger lives in.

03 Confidence · Trust the outcome

Lean on the company's knowledge

The part-time arranger does not know every city or hotel. Show them what colleagues already know. Where the team stayed, what they recommend, what the company prefers. They borrow the company's experience instead of guessing.

Most arrangers do this part time Builds switching cost over time Knowledge feeds policy recommendations
Why this matters

Many arrangers fell into the role in a small company. They book a city they have never visited, with no policy to follow. A tag that shows a colleague stayed here, team recommendations, and company-preferred properties let them lean on what the company already knows. The same data builds an auto-policy starting point, along the lines of "companies like yours spend about this per night."

A "learn from me" function turns one arranger's habits into guidance for others. Each trip adds to what the company knows, which raises switching cost and shortens the next decision. This is a moat that grows on its own, not a single feature.

04 Confidence · Trust the outcome

See the spend, get the invoice

After the trip the arranger chases invoices and reconciles spend by hand. Pull the invoice in one place. See spend against budget. Know the trip stayed in policy. This is the most common reason arrangers churn.

Invoicing: top VoC churn signal 8 mentions, ~82,500 euro HVC at risk Low cost against the churn it stops
Why this matters

Invoicing is the most common churn signal in our voice-of-customer data. There are eight mentions, and one named high-value customer worth about 82,500 euros is flagged at risk. Arrangers chase invoices and stitch expenses together after every trip. Consolidated expenses, fast invoice retrieval, and a group invoice download remove that work.

Spend reporting and spend prediction let the budget owner see where the money goes and what the year will cost. Disruption alerts and traveller tracking add reassurance after booking. The cost to build this is low against the churn it stops, which is why it sits here despite the wider post-booking theme being lower priority.

05 · How we work

Arranger-first by default

This is not a theme. It is how we run the work. Arrangers are too small a share of users to move a blended average, so a change that helps them can look flat across everyone. The fix is to read and prioritise on the arranger cohort, not the whole base. We have started doing this at the feature level. This makes it the standing rule.

Principle Four practices
Experiments
Segment by arranger
Split every experiment result by arranger and self-booker, so we see the effect on the users who drive bookings, not a blended number.
Experiments
Read arrangers first
Judge a change on its arranger result before its overall result. A flat overall number can still hide a strong arranger gain.
Prioritisation
Score by arranger impact
Weight ICE by the effect on arrangers. We have started this at the feature level. It becomes the default across the section.
Prioritisation
Break ties to arrangers
When two items score level, the one that helps arrangers wins. This rule applies in every section, not only this one.

08 · Pre-meeting prep

Questions Bcom will ask. Practise the answers.

Each of these will be asked in some form. How confidently we answer them shows how strong the framing is. Do not enter the meeting without a prepared answer to all six.

You already tag arranger work in every other section. Why a separate section?
The other sections tag the arranger work that sits inside them. This section owns the flows that are arranger-only, like delegation and the trip workspace, which belong nowhere else. It also sets the rule that we measure and prioritise on the arranger first. The lens belongs everywhere. The arranger-only flows and the rule need a home.
Arrangers are only 15% of users. Justify the focus.
They make 82% of our bookings. They activate at 30% against 13% for self-bookers, so the cost per activation is already lower. The top arrangers book 29 times a year against 2.9 for others. Work-email signups, where most arrangers sit, are worth 2.1 times a free-email user. The return per arranger is far higher than per self-booker.
If arrangers are so valuable, why not just acquire more through marketing?
We have tried, and we cannot target them well. Arrangers do not search for travel software for themselves, so paid ads reach self-bookers instead. The product has to do the work that marketing cannot. It finds arrangers in the base we have, turns self-bookers into arrangers through delegation, and keeps the arrangers we have booking.
What is the single metric that ties this together?
Activated arrangers, and bookings per activated arranger. Secondary metrics are the share of bookings made for others, currently about 70%, and the second active users created through delegation. The activation gap, 30% against 13%, is the number we expect to widen as the arranger-only flows land.
Most of these discovery ideas are not built or scored. Is this real for Q3?
The near-term work is the trip workspace and delegation, which connect to Property Details and Trip Management already in flight. Invoicing is small to build and stops a named churn risk, so it earns a place now. The softer ideas, like institutional knowledge and spend prediction, are discovery-backed direction and are sequenced later. We are showing the shape, not committing all of it.
How does this connect to what Bcom invests in on their side?
Two connections. (1) Identity: work-email detection at signup is how we spot a likely arranger, and that touches Bcom-side identity data. (2) Supply: the trip workspace is only worth using if the content breadth is there, which rides Bcom inventory and partnerships. Both are joint roadmap items, not B4B asks.
